FAQ

There are an estimated 300 million people in the world with color vision deficiency. 1 in 12 men are color blind (8%). 1 in 200 women are color blind (0.5%). Red colorblindedness is especially the most common color blind case.

Yes, but it is highly unlikely. Color blindness occurs in only about 1 in 200 women (compared to 1 in 12 men). As a result, approximately 95% of people with color blindness are men. Thanks to chromosomal differences between men and women, color blind women are much fewer and farther between than color blind men. Genetically, it is only possible to have a color blind daughter if both parents are either color blind, or gene carriers.

The cost of EnChroma glasses (most prominent brand in color blind glasses) range from $200-400 for adult pairs, and $269 or more for children. While people with mild to moderate color blindedness significantly benefit from these special glasses, people without any red or green photoreceptors will not be affected by the technology.

Aside from donations -- treat them like normal people. A color blind stated that "The negative reaction of people toward color deficiency can affect colorblind people mental health in a negative way for the whole life." By simply driving conversations away from topics addressing colors, we can create a more inclusive commmunity for the color blind people.
